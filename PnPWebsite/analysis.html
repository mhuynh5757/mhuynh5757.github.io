Literary Exploration by Michael Kitto

Main Point: "Jane Austen plays with the tradition quest format to offer us this rather clever novel."
I agree with the author’s points about the marriages in the book:

  • Mr. and Mrs. Bennet not having a lasting marriage based on long gone lust.
  • Mr. Collins and Charlotte Lucas having a business marriage completely disconnected from emotion.
  • Jane and Bingley being shallowly smitten with each other.
The author makes the point that Elizabeth's marriage is the ideal one, as it was the one forged out of hardship and tempered by violent tempers.
Kitto, Michael. "Pride and Prejudice by Jane Austen." Literary Exploration. 21 Apr. 2013. Web. 29 May 2015.

The Telegraph Review by Victoria Lambert

Main Point: "Jane Austen's classic novel, at 200 years old, is a book without equal, writes Victoria Lambert."
I agreed with the author's points that Jane Austen's desire in writing the novel was making the points about feminism and bring attention to how poorly treated women's rights were. Lambert writes that it is this reality with a hint of Georgian fantasy that keeps readers glued to the pages.

"It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife." 1 Austen is saying that people seek to be financially stable before they decide to look for marriage. However, she is satirizing the fact that people often use financial stability to assert themselves as worthy partners, and that it represents a person as much as their other characteristics, like intelligence or personality. She is also mentioning the fact that her society viewed women as property, instead of individuals, saying that man wants a wife as an object.

She also shows the objectification of women again, only concerned about marrying off her daughters to the nearest rich man.
